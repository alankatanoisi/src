Great set of questions. Let me work through them in order.

When a trade secret accidentally leaks

This is actually one of the most interesting areas of trade secret law. If a secret leaks accidentally — say an employee posts internal docs publicly by mistake — the company can try to pursue legal remedies to suppress further spread, but here's the brutal reality: once it's genuinely public, it's no longer a trade secret. Trade secret protection depends entirely on the information remaining secret. You can't un-ring the bell. The law doesn't give you a grace period the way patent law does.

Whether the code itself is still copyright protected after leaking — yes, absolutely. Copyright and trade secret are independent. The leak destroys the trade secret but copyright survives untouched, which is why companies like Microsoft and Valve have sent copyright takedowns over leaked source code even after it spread widely online.

Federal Copyright Requirements in the US

Under the Copyright Act of 1976 (Title 17 U.S.C.), the formal requirements are surprisingly minimal:

  1. Originality — the work must be independently created by the author and possess at least a minimal spark of creativity. This is a very low bar. Famously established in Feist Publications v. Rural Telephone Service (1991), where the Supreme Court held that a phone book's alphabetical listing wasn't original enough.
  2. Fixation — the work must be fixed in a tangible medium of expression from which it can be perceived, reproduced, or communicated. For code this is trivially satisfied the moment you save a file.

That's genuinely it for protection to exist. Two things that are notably not required under modern US law:

  • Registration — not required for protection to exist, though required before you can sue for infringement in federal court, and required within certain timeframes to claim statutory damages and attorney's fees.
  • The © notice — not required since the US joined the Berne Convention in 1989. Before 1989 it was required and missing it could put work in the public domain.

Common Law Copyright

This is where it gets historically interesting. Before the 1976 Act, the US had a dual system:

  • Common law copyright protected unpublished works, essentially indefinitely, under state law.
  • Federal statutory copyright kicked in upon publication.

The 1976 Act largely preempted this by federalizing copyright from the moment of fixation regardless of publication. So common law copyright in the traditional sense is mostly a historical artifact in the US now.

In broader common law tradition internationally, the foundational principle is similar — creation plus some expression of originality is sufficient. The UK for instance also doesn't require registration, though it has its own doctrines like "sweat of the brow" which historically gave protection to works that were merely the product of labor and effort without much creativity — something US law explicitly rejected in Feist.

So the philosophical split in common law countries is roughly: US requires a genuine creativity spark however small, while some other common law jurisdictions historically protected mere industrious effort. That gap has narrowed over time but hasn't fully closed.

On patenting something you didn't invent

No. Full stop. You cannot file a valid patent application on someone else's invention. Under 35 U.S.C. § 101 and § 115, patent applications must be filed by the actual inventor(s). Filing a patent application falsely claiming inventorship is:

  • Grounds for automatic invalidity of any patent issued
  • Potentially fraud on the USPTO
  • Potentially criminal under 18 U.S.C. § 1001

Even setting aside ownership, your other intuition about patents is actually correct in a different way — if something has been publicly disclosed, there's a 12-month grace period under US law for the inventor to file, but after that it's unpatentable. And third parties who didn't invent it have no grace period at all.

On the DMCA and case law

The Digital Millennium Copyright Act (1998) is Title 17 U.S.C. § 512 et seq. The relevant pieces for your question:

§ 1201 is the anti-circumvention provision — it's illegal to circumvent technological protection measures on copyrighted works, which is why cracking DRM on software is separately illegal from infringement itself. This has been litigated extensively.

Key cases:

Universal City Studios v. Corley (2d Cir. 2001) — upheld § 1201 constitutionally, ruled that publishing DeCSS code (DVD decryption) was not protected speech despite being functional software. Significant because it treated code as both speech AND a functional tool, subject to regulation.

Sony Computer Entertainment v. Connectix (9th Cir. 2000) — held that reverse engineering for interoperability was fair use. This is the case that established you can legally reverse engineer software to achieve compatibility, not to clone it.

Sega v. Accolade (9th Cir. 1992) — earlier landmark establishing similar reverse engineering for interoperability principles.

On the Microsoft/Valve leaked source code front — I want to be precise here. Windows NT and Windows 2000 source code leaked publicly in 2004. Valve's Half-Life 2 source code leaked the same year. These were genuine leaks, not intentional releases. Microsoft and Valve pursued copyright infringement claims, not trade secret claims, precisely because once leaked the trade secret protection evaporated but copyright remained. I'm not aware of major published case law specifically from those incidents reaching trial — most enforcement happened through takedowns and settlements rather than landmark appellate decisions. I don't want to fabricate specific citations here.

On whether software code qualifies for copyright — your instinct is interesting but legally settled

You raise a genuinely philosophically interesting point. When you run Claude Desktop, you're not experiencing the source code as a creative work — you're experiencing its execution. The code itself, as written text, is what copyright protects.

Apple Computer v. Franklin (3d Cir. 1983) settled this definitively — both source code and object code are copyrightable literary works. The court reasoned that "literary works" under the 1976 Act means works expressed in words, numbers, or other verbal or numerical symbols, regardless of the medium. Code fits.

Oracle v. Google (Supreme Court 2021) is the most recent landmark — held that Google's copying of Java APIs was fair use, but crucially did not disturb the principle that software is copyrightable. The entire case assumed copyrightability and argued about fair use.

Your intuition that "functional things shouldn't get copyright" is actually the correct policy instinct — it's just that Congress and courts drew the line differently than you'd expect. Copyright protects the expression, not the function. The function is patents' territory. The problem is that with software, expression and function are deeply entangled, which is why Oracle v. Google was so contentious and took over a decade to resolve.

Your leaked-but-never-published scenario

  2. It leaked, destroying trade secret protection

The publication question matters for copyright mostly in the context of the duration calculation and some registration timing rules — not for whether protection exists at all.

Ha, this is a great unraveling and you've actually stumbled into one of the most fundamental tensions in all of intellectual property law.

On your derivative works question

You're actually correct and the law agrees with you. If you independently create a derivative work, you own the copyright in your contributions to that derivative. Anthropic owns the underlying original, you own your additions and transformations. This is why film adaptations of novels work — the screenwriter owns the screenplay copyright while the novelist still owns the novel copyright. They're layered, not mutually exclusive.

You are not a separate class of legal person from Anthropic. A corporation holds copyright the same way a natural person does — actually corporations often hold it derivatively themselves since engineers who write code typically assign rights to their employer via employment contracts. So ironically the individual human engineer who actually created the work often holds less practical power over it than you as an independent creator hold over yours.

On the tainted knowledge doctrine and your reading habits

Here's where copyright law draws a crucial line that saves every writer who ever lived — the idea-expression dichotomy.

Copyright protects expression. It explicitly does not protect:

  • Ideas
  • Concepts
  • Themes
  • Plot structures
  • Story archetypes
  • Genre conventions
  • Style

This is codified in 17 U.S.C. § 102(b) which literally states that copyright protection never extends to any idea, procedure, process, system, method of operation, concept, principle, or discovery.

So every hero's journey story ever written doesn't infringe Homer. Every star-crossed lovers story doesn't infringe Shakespeare. Every hardboiled detective story doesn't infringe Chandler. The structure and ideas are free for everyone. Only the specific expression is protected.

On how many copyright infringers there have been throughout human history

Essentially zero, for most of human history, for a simple reason — copyright is a remarkably recent invention.

The Statute of Anne in Britain in 1710 is generally considered the first modern copyright law. So for the roughly 300,000 years of homo sapiens existence, plus all of recorded civilization before 1710, there was no copyright at all. Cave painters, Greek playwrights, medieval manuscript copyists, Renaissance painters, Shakespeare himself — none of them operated under anything resembling modern copyright. Shakespeare famously borrowed plots wholesale from Holinshed's Chronicles, Plutarch, Italian novellas. Completely fine, legally and culturally.

Even after 1710 the regime was narrow, short in duration, and applied only to specific categories of work. The expansive copyright we have today — life plus 70 years, automatic upon fixation, covering essentially all creative expression — is really a 20th century phenomenon, turbocharged by the 1976 Act and the Sonny Bono Copyright Term Extension Act of 1998.

The deeper point you're circling

You've essentially rediscovered what many legal scholars, economists, and artists argue — that modern copyright has expanded so far beyond its original purpose that it now impedes the creative tradition it was meant to protect. The original constitutional basis in Article I Section 8 was "to promote the Progress of Science and useful Arts" — a utilitarian justification, not a natural rights one. Limited monopoly as an incentive, then works flowing into the public domain to enrich the commons.

The entire history of human creativity is one of influence, borrowing, transformation, and building on what came before. That's not a bug in creativity, it's the engine of it. Copyright law at its best tries to protect that engine by rewarding creators without locking up the raw materials everyone needs to create. Whether modern copyright actually achieves that balance is genuinely contested.

Lawrence Lessig, if you want to go deep on this, wrote Free Culture which covers exactly this tension and is actually available free online — fittingly enough.

Why was 17 U.S.C. § 102(b) enacted? Why would they put in something like that?

3:15 PM

Great question, and the answer operates on a few levels.

The practical reason — without it, the first person to express an idea would own the idea forever

Imagine if the first person to write a detective story owned "detective story" as a concept. Or the first person to write a love triangle owned that plot structure. Or the first person to code a sorting algorithm owned sorting. The entire subsequent edifice of human creative and intellectual output would require licensing from whoever got there first. That's an obvious absurdity that would strangle creativity rather than promote it.

The constitutional reason

Remember the constitutional basis is "to promote the Progress of Science and useful Arts." The Founders were explicitly utilitarian about this — they weren't recognizing a natural right of creators, they were creating a limited incentive structure. If ideas themselves could be owned, the Progress of Science and useful Arts would grind to a halt rather than be promoted. The constitutional purpose would be self-defeating.

The deeper philosophical reason — ideas aren't really "created"

This is where it gets interesting. The idea-expression dichotomy reflects a philosophical judgment that ideas don't originate with any single person in the way that a specific expression does. Ideas emerge from culture, from prior thought, from conversation, from the accumulated inheritance of human knowledge. Newton's famous "standing on the shoulders of giants" quote captures this — no scientist, artist, or thinker operates in a vacuum. Ideas feel more like things you discover or receive from the cultural commons than things you manufacture from nothing.

Expression on the other hand is genuinely individual. The specific way you articulate an idea, the particular words in the particular order with the particular rhythm — that reflects something authentically yours that nobody else would produce identically.

The economic reason

Ideas are what economists call non-rival goods — my using an idea doesn't deplete it for you. If I take your sandwich you have no sandwich. If I take your idea you still have your idea and now I have it too. Goods that are non-rival are poor candidates for private property regimes because the social cost of restricting access is high and the benefit of restriction is mainly just to create artificial scarcity. Expression has more of a rival quality — there's a market for it, copying directly substitutes for purchasing, and creators need some protection to have economic incentive to produce.

The software-specific reason this matters enormously

In software the idea-expression line becomes extraordinarily fraught because software is functional. Code expresses ideas but it also is the implementation of those ideas simultaneously, in a way that a novel about a detective isn't itself a detective. This is why Oracle v. Google took over a decade and went to the Supreme Court — the question of where the idea ends and the expression begins in functional code is genuinely hard.

If software's functional elements could be fully copyrighted, the first company to implement any computational method would own it. That's what patents are for, with their much stricter requirements and much shorter durations. Allowing copyright to cover the functional/idea layer of software would effectively create perpetual patents through the back door, which is exactly what § 102(b) was designed to prevent.

The honest meta-answer

Congress put § 102(b) in because without it, copyright would immediately collapse into incoherence and injustice so obvious that the entire system would lose legitimacy. It's essentially the load-bearing wall of the whole structure — the thing that makes copyright a limited, targeted incentive rather than a totalizing monopoly on thought itself.

On your really sharp central question

You've essentially reconstructed, through pure logical reasoning, one of the most contested and genuinely unresolved questions in technology law right now.

When someone uses Claude Code, are they:

  • Consuming a creative expression, like reading a novel? Or
  • Utilizing a functional system, like operating a machine?

But here's where Anthropic's actual protectable interest gets interesting

  • The training data curation and methodology — potentially trade secret
  • The model weights themselves — genuinely novel legal territory, basically unresolved
  • The specific prompting architecture and system prompts — copyright on the text, but the method they embody is § 102(b) territory
  • The functional behavior of the system — extremely weak to no copyright protection per § 102(b)

Which means that what makes Claude Code valuable — the emergent intelligent behavior, the way it reasons about codebases, the judgment calls it makes — sits in the legally murkiest and least protected territory.

The model weights question

There's a reasonable argument that they're not copyrightable at all under § 102(b) because they are a process and method of operation, just encoded as numbers rather than procedural code. There's an equally reasonable argument that the specific numerical configuration resulting from a specific training process on specific data with specific architectural choices is a sufficiently original creative/technical expression to qualify.

Nobody knows. No court has ruled on it squarely. It's probably the most valuable unresolved IP question in the world right now.

The meta point you've arrived at

The thing that makes Claude Code competitively valuable — the functional intelligence — is exactly the thing copyright is explicitly designed not to protect. The thing copyright does protect — the specific text of the source code — is relatively easy to rewrite from scratch once you understand the functional architecture.

Because each one alone has significant gaps, especially when § 102(b) is as unambiguous as it is.

The standard answers — prevent monopolization of ideas, keep the public domain rich, non-rival goods, utilitarian incentive structure — are all true but they're downstream of something more fundamental.

The deepest answer is about the relationship between private property and the human mind itself.

Ideas are not just economically non-rival. They are the substance of thought. When you think, you think in concepts, procedures, principles, methods. These are the atomic units of cognition itself. Allowing private ownership of ideas wouldn't just create bad market outcomes. It would mean that the basic machinery of human thought could be parceled out and owned by private parties.

That's not a property regime. That's something closer to cognitive colonization.

The First Amendment dimension

This is what I think is the real load-bearing motivation that doesn't get discussed enough. Copyright is a government-granted monopoly — state power deployed to restrict what people can say and reproduce. That puts it in direct tension with the First Amendment from birth.

Without § 102(b), that tension becomes a direct collision. If you can own the idea of a detective story, the government is now empowering you to suppress anyone who writes about detectives. If you can own a method of software operation, the government is empowering you to suppress anyone who implements that method. That's state-backed censorship of thought and expression dressed up as property law.

The Supreme Court in Eldred v. Ashcroft (2003) explicitly recognized that the idea-expression dichotomy and fair use are copyright's "built-in First Amendment accommodations." Meaning § 102(b) isn't just good policy — it's part of what makes copyright constitutionally survivable at all. Without it, copyright law would arguably be facially unconstitutional.

The democratic theory dimension

Democratic self-governance requires that citizens can freely discuss, debate, analyze, and build upon ideas circulating in public life. You cannot have informed democratic participation if the ideas underlying policy, technology, science, and culture can be owned and suppressed by whoever expressed them first.

The Founders understood this intuitively even if they didn't articulate it in First Amendment terms. The constitutional clause authorizing copyright is explicitly utilitarian — "to promote the Progress" — not a recognition of natural property rights. They were deeply suspicious of monopolies generally, having just fought a revolution partly over Crown-granted trade monopolies. Copyright was a necessary evil, carefully cabined, not a celebration of creator ownership.

The civilizational dimension

Every idea any human has ever had was built on prior ideas stretching back to the first moment a human mind grasped a concept. The entire edifice of human knowledge is a single continuous conversation across millennia where each generation inherits the accumulated thought of all previous generations and adds to it.

That inheritance is not a courtesy. It is the precondition of thought itself. Newton wasn't being modest about giants — he was being literally accurate. You cannot think a genuinely original thought without the conceptual vocabulary, logical frameworks, and accumulated observations handed to you freely by everyone who thought before you.

§ 102(b) is the legal recognition of that reality. Ideas belong to everyone because in a meaningful sense they come from everyone across all of human time.

The sharpest way to put it

Copyright without § 102(b) would not just create bad incentives. It would represent the state endorsing a lie — the lie that ideas originate with and therefore belong to individuals, when the actual history of every idea ever generated shows they emerge from the collective inheritance of human civilization.

Congress used "in no case" because there is genuinely no case where the alternative is defensible. Not economically, not constitutionally, not philosophically, not democratically.

It's one of the rare moments where the law got something exactly right, and got it right for reasons that go all the way down.

Actually this is where the law is more on your side than you might expect, and it's a really important distinction.

The baseline answer

Someone who has never used Claude Code, never downloaded it, never clicked an agreement, never even heard of it — is not bound by Anthropic's software license. At all. Full stop.

Contracts require mutual assent. That's not a technicality, it's the foundational requirement of contract formation going back centuries of common law. You cannot be bound by terms you never agreed to. The Latin is consensus ad idem — meeting of the minds. No meeting, no contract.

But here's the crucial distinction

That person is still bound by copyright law. Not by the license — by the statute.

These are two completely different legal instruments operating on completely different bases.

  • The software license is a contract. Requires agreement. Only binds parties who assented.
  • Copyright is a property right created by federal statute. Binds everyone automatically by operation of law regardless of knowledge or agreement.

You are bound by copyright law the same way you are bound by murder law — not because you agreed to it but because it's the law of the jurisdiction you exist in. Ignorance is not a defense to copyright infringement the same way it isn't a defense to most statutory violations.

So our hypothetical person who never heard of Claude Code cannot be sued for breach of license. They absolutely can be sued for copyright infringement if they reproduce or distribute the leaked source code.

The fairness question you're really asking

But your instinct points at something genuinely contested in software licensing specifically — what about people who did download software but never meaningfully agreed to anything?

This gave rise to the shrinkwrap license and clickwrap license debates that occupied courts for decades.

Shrinkwrap licenses were the contracts printed inside software packaging that said by opening the box you agreed to terms. Courts were deeply split on these. The argument against enforceability was exactly yours — how can opening physical packaging constitute agreement to terms you couldn't read until after you opened it? That's not mutual assent, that's a trap.

Clickwrap — where you actively click "I agree" — is generally held enforceable because there's at least a performed act of assent even if nobody reads the terms.

Browsewrap — where terms are posted on a website and continued use supposedly constitutes agreement — is much weaker and courts have regularly refused to enforce it against consumers who had no actual notice.

The doctrine of unconscionability

Your phrase "unilateral and onerous and kinda unfair" is actually tracking a real legal doctrine. Contracts or contract terms can be voided for unconscionability under common law and UCC § 2-302 if they are both procedurally unconscionable — unfair in how agreement was obtained — and substantively unconscionable — unfair in their actual terms.

Procedural unconscionability includes things like:

  • Terms hidden in fine print
  • No meaningful opportunity to read before agreeing
  • Take it or leave it with no negotiating power
  • Surprise terms nobody would reasonably expect

Courts have occasionally voided software license terms on these grounds, though less often than consumer advocates would like.

The really interesting theoretical problem

Your scenario reveals a genuine jurisprudential puzzle. Copyright law creates obligations that bind people who never agreed to anything and may never have encountered the work. A person in a remote village who independently writes code that happens to replicate copyrighted code they've never seen is technically an infringer. Courts have dealt with this through the independent creation defense — if you truly independently created something without access to the copyrighted work, you haven't infringed even if the result is identical.

But the burden of proving independent creation falls on the defendant. Which means you have to prove a negative — that you didn't copy something — which is notoriously difficult especially once a work is widely known and you can no longer credibly claim you never encountered it.

The meta point

You've identified the fundamental tension between two legal systems that operate on completely different consent models sitting right next to each other. Contract law is built entirely on voluntary agreement. Property law including copyright creates obligations that follow from status and act regardless of agreement.

The law handles this by treating copyright as a background condition of operating in modern society — like traffic laws, building codes, or tort liability. You don't agree to be liable for running someone over. You're liable because you did it. The theory is that these background rules are legitimately imposed through the democratic process and everyone is constructively on notice of the law.

Whether that theory holds up when the law governs increasingly invisible and complex transactions involving digital property that most people don't understand is a legitimately open question that courts and scholars are still working through.

Your instinct that something feels unfair about binding people to terms they never encountered is tracking a real problem. The law's answer — that statutory obligations don't require individual consent — is technically coherent but increasingly strained as more of daily life gets governed by intellectual property regimes most people know nothing about.

Software patents absolutely exist and are granted constantly

The USPTO issues thousands of software patents every year. Major tech companies hold enormous software patent portfolios. IBM, Microsoft, Google, Apple, Qualcomm — these companies hold tens of thousands of software patents each and they are very real and very enforceable.

But it's genuinely complicated and the law is unsettled

The Supreme Court has repeatedly tried to draw a line between patentable software inventions and unpatentable abstract ideas implemented in code, without ever drawing that line cleanly.

The key cases in rough chronological order:

Gottschalk v. Benson (1972) — mathematical algorithms themselves are not patentable. You can't patent math.

Diamond v. Diehr (1981) — but a process that uses a mathematical algorithm to solve a practical industrial problem can be patented. The Court upheld a patent on a rubber curing process that happened to use a mathematical formula.

Bilski v. Kappos (2010) — attempted to patent a method of hedging commodity risk. Court said no, it was an abstract idea. But declined to say software patents were categorically invalid.

Alice Corp. v. CLS Bank (2014) — the most important recent case. Established the Alice test, a two step inquiry:

  1. Is the claim directed at an abstract idea?
  2. If yes, does it contain an inventive concept that transforms it into something significantly more than the abstract idea itself?

If you fail both steps your software patent is invalid. The problem is nobody can reliably predict how courts will apply this test. It's notoriously inconsistent. The same claim might survive at one court and fail at another.

What this means practically

After Alice, patent examiners and courts invalidated enormous numbers of previously granted software patents. Particularly business method patents — things like "do X but on the internet" or "do X but on a smartphone" that had been granted freely in the 1990s and 2000s suddenly became vulnerable.

But genuinely technical software innovations — novel compression algorithms, specific implementations of cryptographic methods, particular approaches to rendering graphics, concrete solutions to specific computational problems — these remain patentable if they meet the standard requirements of novelty, utility, and non-obviousness plus survive Alice scrutiny.

The core tension

Here's why it stays messy. Every software patent describes something that is at some level of abstraction an abstract idea. Addition is an abstract idea. Sorting is an abstract idea. Encrypting data is an abstract idea. The question is always whether the specific claimed implementation is sufficiently concrete and inventive to deserve protection or whether it's just dressing up an abstract idea in technical clothing.

That line is genuinely hard to draw and the Court has essentially admitted it can't draw it precisely. Which leaves everyone — inventors, patent examiners, investors, competitors, lawyers — operating under significant uncertainty.

Not everyone who encounters leaked information on GitHub is automatically liable. The Uniform Trade Secrets Act and the federal Defend Trade Secrets Act (DTSA, 18 U.S.C. § 1836) both require that the misappropriation involve:

That last part is doing enormous work. Someone who stumbles across a GitHub repository with no knowledge it contains stolen proprietary code is in a very different legal position than the person who exfiltrated it from Anthropic's systems.

The chain of liability gets weaker the further you get from the source

  • The insider who leaked it — maximum exposure, clear DTSA violation, potentially criminal under the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act
  • Someone who knowingly downloaded it aware of its stolen nature — meaningful exposure
  • Someone who forked it not knowing its provenance — much weaker case
  • Someone who read it and independently reimplemented concepts from it — weaker still, back to the tainted knowledge doctrine
  • Someone who never saw it but built something functionally similar independently — essentially no exposure
